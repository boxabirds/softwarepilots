# Workshop Simulation: Software Pilots Critical Review

**Date:** March 11, 2026
**Format:** Simulated multi-round workshop
**Panellists:** Martin Fowler (software design), John Carmack (systems engineering), Sal Khan (mastery-based education, Khan Academy), Barbara Oakley (cognitive science of learning, Learning How to Learn)
**Input documents:** tech-architecture.md (Baseline v0.1), content-strategy.md (Baseline v0.1), curriculum.md (v0.2), 20260309-chat.md (session notes)
**Question:** "The Software Pilots project is committed to world class outcomes and we want some critical thinking around the approach to curriculum and the technical architecture. What is good and what should change?"

---

## Round 1: Initial Reactions

### Martin Fowler

"I'll start with what's good. The three-layer curriculum architecture — pilotry-native, applied foundations, reference labs — is a genuinely sound separation of concerns. It mirrors what I've always advocated in software: separate the things that change at different rates. Layer 3 changes quarterly, Layer 1 changes annually. That's good architecture applied to content.

But I have a structural concern with the technical architecture. You've written a remarkably detailed Baseline v0.1 — eleven content types, a full data model, a cost model per exercise. This is big upfront design. Where's your walking skeleton? You teach decomposition and walking skeletons in Module 4 but your own architecture doesn't follow the principle. I'd want to see the thinnest possible end-to-end slice — one learner, one exercise, one evaluation — working before you build out eleven content types.

The eleven content types themselves worry me. CT-1 through CT-11 feels like a premature taxonomy. In my experience, you discover your real categories by building, not by classifying upfront. I'd bet that by the time you ship, three or four of these collapse into each other and two you didn't anticipate emerge. Start with CT-1 (narrative), CT-2 (sandbox), CT-6 (structured authoring with AI eval), and CT-7 (agent build). Those four cover 80% of the curriculum. Add the others when you have a learner in front of you telling you what's missing."

### John Carmack

"The client-side execution strategy is exactly right. Sandpack, sql.js in WASM — zero marginal cost, instant feedback, no server round-trip. That's how you get tight iteration loops. Don't let anyone talk you out of this for the early modules.

The stochastic moment exercise — submitting the same spec five times and comparing outputs — is the single best exercise in this entire curriculum. It's concrete, visceral, and unforgettable. It creates the kind of permanent intuition that no amount of lecturing achieves. The compiler moment is good too, but it's teaching something most people already vaguely know. The stochastic moment teaches something most people actively get wrong.

My concern is the Container tier. This is where your cost model goes from dollars to potentially hundreds of dollars per learner. And honestly, I'd question whether you need it at all for a foundation course. The bring-your-own-tools path for Module 6 should be the *primary* path, not the fallback. Every learner who takes this course should be using a real agent tool in their real environment — that's the whole point. You're teaching pilotry, not hosting. If you build a VibeSDK fork with custom containers, you're now maintaining infrastructure that isn't your product. Your product is the curriculum and the evaluation. Ship the evaluator, let the learner use whatever agent they want, and save yourself a massive ops burden.

Also — 29 to 36 hours. What's the minimum viable course? If I had to cut this to 12 hours, what survives? Modules 2, 3, 4, and 8. The compiler moment, the stochastic moment, specification, and verification. That's the spine. Everything else is valuable but not essential for the first cohort."

### Sal Khan

"I want to talk about practice density. I've been looking at the exercise count per module, and it's thin. Module 3 has two exercises across four hours. Module 5 has one exercise across three hours. At Khan Academy, we'd have ten to fifteen practice problems per concept. I understand these aren't math problems — they're complex, open-ended tasks — but the ratio of content-to-practice is heavily skewed toward content.

The deeper issue is mastery gating. Your architecture has a `progress` table with status tracking, but the content strategy describes a linear sequence with no gates. Can a learner skip Module 4 and go straight to Module 6? The architecture doesn't prevent it, and the content strategy doesn't address it. If specification is the pilot's primary skill — your words — then Module 6 should be locked until Module 4 is demonstrably passed. Otherwise you're building the thing you're teaching people not to build: software without verification.

The tutor agent is a good idea but it's positioned wrong. You describe it as something the learner consults after failing — 'Why did my specification fail?' It should be available *during* the exercise. The tightest feedback loop is: learner writes a sentence of specification, tutor gives inline feedback, learner revises. Not: learner writes an entire specification, submits, waits for scoring, reads feedback, tries to remember what they were thinking when they wrote paragraph three. Khanmigo works because it's there in the moment, not after the fact."

### Barbara Oakley

"The two pedagogical anchors — the compiler moment and the stochastic moment — are excellent. They're what I'd call 'chunk creators.' They produce a dense, memorable, emotionally loaded unit of understanding that the learner can build on. The prediction-then-surprise structure of the compiler moment is textbook desirable difficulty. The five-outputs-side-by-side structure of the stochastic moment creates genuine cognitive dissonance. Both are well-designed.

But here's what's missing from the entire architecture: spaced repetition and interleaving. The curriculum is purely sequential. Module 2 is taught, tested, and never revisited. Module 3 is taught, tested, and never revisited. By the time the learner reaches Module 8, the compiler moment is six modules ago. The research is unambiguous on this: massed practice — doing all your learning on a topic in one block — produces fast initial performance and rapid forgetting. Interleaved practice — mixing topics across sessions — produces slower initial performance and durable retention.

Concretely: Module 6 should include a retrieval exercise that forces the learner to recall Module 2 concepts without re-reading them. Module 8 should require the learner to re-derive their Module 4 specification principles from scratch, not just apply a checklist. The final project does some of this naturally, but by then the forgetting curve has already done its damage.

I'm also concerned about cognitive load in Module 4. Five hours of specification writing is demanding, and the module's own content acknowledges this — there's a note about fatigue degrading specification quality. But the solution isn't a note. The solution is structural: break Module 4 into two sessions with a mandatory gap. Or interleave it with Module 5, which is lighter and more conceptual, to give the specification muscles time to consolidate."

## Round 2: Deep Dives

### Fowler pushes on evaluator architecture

"The evaluator is the riskiest component in this system and it's treated as a solved problem. You define rubrics as structured JSON with dimensions, weights, and pass thresholds. Then you send the submission plus rubric to an AI model and parse the score. But AI evaluation of open-ended work is not a parsing problem — it's a calibration problem.

How do you know the evaluator is scoring consistently? How do you know it's scoring *correctly*? You mention 'testing against 10+ sample submissions before going live.' Ten is not enough. You need a calibration corpus of at least 50 submissions per exercise, scored by human experts, and you need to measure evaluator agreement with those expert scores continuously. When the evaluator drifts — and it will, especially when you change models or rubric versions — you need to catch it.

The content strategy mentions 25 rubrics. That's 25 x 50 = 1,250 expert-scored sample submissions you need before you can trust the evaluator. Have you budgeted for that? Who does the expert scoring? This is the hidden cost that will determine whether your AI evaluation is 'world class' or 'good enough to ship but occasionally embarrassing.'

I'd also add an escape valve: when the evaluator scores below a confidence threshold, route to human review. Don't pretend AI evaluation is binary. Your premium tier mentions optional human pilot review — make it automatic for low-confidence evaluations, not just available for learners who pay more."

### Carmack challenges the sandbox approach for Module 2

"The compiler moment uses Sandpack with five lines of JavaScript. But I want to push on whether JavaScript is the right language for this. The goal is to make the learner *feel the machine* — to understand that instructions are precise and literal. JavaScript is precisely the wrong language for that lesson. It's loosely typed, it coerces types silently, `'5' + 3` gives you `'53'` and `5 + '3'` gives you `'53'` too but for a different reason. The learner's prediction step will fail not because they don't understand the machine but because JavaScript's semantics are surprising even to experienced programmers.

If you want the compiler moment to land — if you want the learner to walk away understanding that the machine does exactly what it's told — use a language where the machine does exactly what it's told. Python would be better. A constrained subset of TypeScript with strict mode would be better still — the type system catches errors at the 'instruction' level, which reinforces the precision lesson. You have Pyodide noted as an option in the session notes. I'd seriously consider it.

The trade-off is that JavaScript gives you Sandpack for free and everything downstream (DevTools, Network tab, browser rendering) is JavaScript-native. That's a real advantage. But the compiler moment is the *foundational exercise of the entire course*. If it teaches the wrong lesson because of JavaScript's quirks, you're building on a cracked foundation."

### Khan on the feedback loop architecture

"Let me be specific about what I mean by tighter feedback. Your CT-6 content type — structured authoring — has this flow: learner writes a full document, submits it, evaluator scores it, learner reads feedback. That's a batch feedback loop. It's the essay-grading model. It works, but it's not how people learn most effectively.

What works better is what I'd call progressive evaluation. The learner writes the first acceptance criterion. The tutor agent immediately responds: 'This criterion mentions the system should be fast. What's the measurable threshold? What's the failure case?' The learner revises. Then writes the second criterion. The tutor evaluates that one immediately. By the time the learner has written five criteria, they've received five rounds of feedback and course-corrected in real time.

This doesn't replace the final AI evaluation — you still submit the full document and get a comprehensive score. But it changes the learning dynamic from 'write everything, submit, find out you were wrong' to 'learn the pattern through guided iteration.'

Architecturally, this means the tutor Durable Object needs to be active during CT-6 exercises, not just available on demand. It needs access to the rubric so it can give rubric-aligned feedback in real time. And the CT-6 content type needs a 'guided mode' where the editor and tutor are side by side.

This is more expensive per learner — you're making inference calls per paragraph instead of per submission. But it's where the learning actually happens. The batch evaluation at the end becomes a confirmation, not a surprise."

### Oakley on the missing metacognitive layer

"There's a concept missing from this curriculum that should be threaded through every module: metacognitive monitoring. The learner needs to be taught — explicitly — how to monitor their own understanding. Not just 'do you understand this?' but 'what specifically would change if you were wrong about this?'

This connects directly to the cognitive surrender research you've added to Module 3. Shaw and Nave found that people with lower need for cognition surrendered more to AI outputs. But need for cognition isn't fixed — it can be trained. The training is metacognitive: teaching people to notice when they're accepting something without examining it.

Every exercise should have a metacognitive checkpoint. After the learner submits their work, before they see the score, ask: 'How confident are you in this submission? Which dimension do you think is weakest? What would you change if you had 10 more minutes?' Then show the score. The gap between their prediction and the actual score *is the learning*. It's exactly the same principle as the compiler moment — predict, then discover reality — but applied to their own competence.

Your data model captures scores but not confidence predictions. Add a `self_assessment_json` column to the `submissions` table. Over time, you can measure whether learners' self-assessment accuracy improves across the course. That's a direct measure of metacognitive development — and it's the single best predictor of whether they'll be good pilots. A pilot who knows what they don't know is safe. A pilot who doesn't know what they don't know is dangerous."

## Round 3: Cross-Pollination and Debate

### Carmack responds to Khan

"I take Sal's point about progressive evaluation, but I want to push back on the cost implication. He's proposing inference calls per paragraph. For a five-hour module with 15 exercises, that could be 50-100 inference calls per learner just for Module 4. Your cost model estimates $3-8 per learner for the full course. Progressive evaluation in Module 4 alone could double that.

Here's an alternative: use the tutor agent for progressive evaluation but run it on a cheaper, faster model. The final evaluation uses a frontier model for accuracy. The inline tutor uses a smaller model for speed and cost. The tutor doesn't need to be right every time — it needs to be *useful* every time. 'Have you considered the failure case?' is a useful prompt even if the model couldn't score the criterion accurately. The evaluator needs to be accurate. The tutor needs to be provocative."

### Khan responds to Carmack

"That's exactly right. And it maps to how human tutoring works — the tutor asks questions, the exam gives scores. Different functions, different quality bars. I'd add: the tutor's prompts can be partially templated. For acceptance criteria, you don't need a frontier model to ask 'What's the measurable threshold?' That's a pattern-match on the rubric dimensions. Save the expensive inference for the final evaluation."

### Fowler responds to Oakley

"Barbara's point about self-assessment connects to something I've seen in software teams for decades. The best engineers are the ones who can accurately estimate their own confidence in a piece of code. 'I'm 90% sure this handles the edge cases' versus 'I think this works but I haven't thought about concurrency.' That skill — calibrated self-assessment — is exactly what a software pilot needs. And you're right that the architecture doesn't capture it.

But I'd go further. The self-assessment data isn't just a learning signal for the individual learner. It's a *product signal* for the curriculum. If learners consistently overestimate their confidence on Module 4 exercises and underestimate on Module 8, that tells you Module 4's feedback isn't landing and Module 8's is. You can use the Dunning-Kruger gap data across your entire learner population to identify which modules need redesign. That's a feedback loop that most educational platforms don't have."

### Oakley responds to Carmack on the 12-hour minimum

"John suggested cutting to Modules 2, 3, 4, and 8 for a 12-hour minimum course. I'd push back on removing Module 1. Without the framing — why this matters, the accountability question, the three roles — the learner has no motivation framework. The compiler moment is impressive but meaningless without context. It takes two hours. Keep it.

But I'd support cutting Module 5 from the minimum viable course. The autonomy spectrum is important for practitioners but it's conceptual knowledge, not a skill. You can teach it as a reading assignment with a comprehension check, not a full module. That gives you: Module 1 (2h), Module 2 (4h), Module 3 (4h), Module 4 (5h — or 3h if you trim), Module 8 (3h). That's 16-18 hours. Still long, but defensible."

### Carmack pushes back

"Fair point on Module 1. I'll concede that. But I stand by the 12-hour target. Five hours on specification is too long for a *foundation* course. The learner needs to understand that specification matters and practice it once or twice. They don't need six exercises. Cut 4.4 (decomposition), 4.5 (constraints), and 4.6 (specification for different audiences) from the minimum path. Keep 4.1 (problem before solution), 4.2 (testable criteria), and 4.3 (edge cases). That's the core skill in three exercises, maybe 2.5 hours. Total: Module 1 (2h) + Module 2 (3h, cut the SQL exercise) + Module 3 (3h, cut model selection) + Module 4 (2.5h) + Module 8 (2h, cut the checklist detail). That's 12.5 hours. A weekend intensive."

### Khan on the weekend intensive

"A weekend intensive is a different product with a different pedagogy. Self-paced over weeks allows for spaced repetition and consolidation. A weekend intensive requires a completely different exercise design — higher scaffolding, more guided practice, less open-ended exploration. Don't confuse cutting content with changing format. If you offer both, design them separately."

## Round 4: Concrete Recommendations

### Martin Fowler — Top 3

1. **Build the walking skeleton first.** One module (Module 2), one exercise (the compiler moment), one evaluation (did they predict correctly?), one learner, deployed to Cloudflare. Validate the entire pipeline before building content types 3 through 11. You have 11 content types defined and zero working. Invert that ratio.

2. **Invest in evaluator calibration as a first-class workstream.** Budget for a corpus of 50+ expert-scored submissions per exercise. Measure evaluator-expert agreement. Add a confidence score to every evaluation and route low-confidence results to human review automatically, not as a premium add-on.

3. **Collapse content types.** Start with four: narrative (CT-1), sandbox (CT-2), structured authoring with AI eval (CT-6), and bring-your-own-tools build (CT-7 without the container path). Add others when a real learner demonstrates the need.

### John Carmack — Top 3

1. **Kill the container path for v1.** Make bring-your-own-tools the primary and only path for agent-assisted builds. Your product is the curriculum and the evaluator, not a hosting platform. This eliminates your highest-cost infrastructure component and your biggest ops risk. Revisit containers when you have revenue and a devops team.

2. **Reconsider JavaScript for the compiler moment.** Test the five-line exercise with 10 non-technical people using JavaScript and 10 using Python. Measure which group more accurately describes what the machine did and why. Let the data decide. If JavaScript's type coercion creates confusion that obscures the lesson, switch to Python via Pyodide. The downstream cost (DevTools exercises need adaptation) is real but manageable.

3. **Design a 12-hour minimum viable course** as a distinct product. Modules 1, 2, 3, core of 4, and core of 8. No containers, no simulation exercises, no dashboard interpretation. This ships first, generates feedback, and the full 30-hour course evolves from it.

### Sal Khan — Top 3

1. **Add mastery gates between modules.** Module 4 requires passing Module 2 and 3. Module 6 requires passing Module 4. Module 8 requires passing Module 6. The `progress` table already supports this — add a `prerequisite_check` to the Course API Worker. Non-negotiable for a course that teaches verification to let learners skip verification of their own learning.

2. **Redesign CT-6 with progressive inline feedback.** The tutor agent should be active during structured authoring exercises, giving sentence-level or paragraph-level feedback in real time. Use a cheaper model for inline tutoring, reserve the frontier model for final evaluation. This is the highest-leverage change for learning outcomes.

3. **Triple the exercise count.** 25 exercises across 30 hours is less than one per hour. Add lightweight retrieval exercises — five-minute checks that don't require AI evaluation. 'Write one testable acceptance criterion for this scenario.' 'Identify the failure mode in this agent configuration.' 'What status code would you expect?' These can be self-checked or assertion-evaluated. They cost nothing and they're where the learning consolidates.

### Barbara Oakley — Top 3

1. **Add metacognitive self-assessment to every exercise.** Before seeing their score, learners predict their performance and identify their weakest dimension. Store predictions in the data model. Measure calibration improvement across the course. This is both a learning intervention and a product metric — and it directly trains the 'knowing what you don't know' skill that defines a good pilot.

2. **Interleave modules structurally.** After Module 4, include a 15-minute 'retrieval sprint' that asks three questions from Modules 2 and 3 without allowing the learner to review the material. After Module 6, include a sprint covering Modules 3 and 4. The discomfort of retrieval without review is the mechanism that produces durable learning. It will feel harder to learners but the evidence is unambiguous that it works.

3. **Break Module 4 into two sessions with a mandatory 24-hour gap.** The first session covers 4.1-4.3 (problem definition, acceptance criteria, edge cases). The mandatory gap allows consolidation — the diffuse mode of thinking where connections form. The second session covers 4.4-4.6 (decomposition, constraints, audience adaptation). This practices what Module 9 preaches about sustainable practice. If the course doesn't enforce its own principles in its own structure, the principles ring hollow.

## Synthesis: What Emerged

### Unanimous agreement
- The two pedagogical anchors (compiler moment, stochastic moment) are strong and should be protected
- The evaluator is under-designed relative to its importance — calibration is critical
- Client-side execution for early modules is the right call
- The course needs more frequent, lighter-weight practice alongside the major exercises

### Majority agreement (3/4)
- Containers should be deferred or eliminated for v1
- Module 4 is too long as a single block
- A minimum viable course (12-16 hours) should be designed as a distinct product
- The tutor agent should be active during exercises, not just after

### Productive disagreement
- **JavaScript vs. Python for Module 2**: Carmack wants to test alternatives, Fowler thinks the DevTools dependency justifies JavaScript, Khan and Oakley are agnostic on language but care about the prediction-surprise structure working
- **12 hours vs. 16 hours for minimum course**: Carmack pushes 12, Oakley insists Module 1 framing is non-negotiable, Khan argues a weekend intensive is a different pedagogical product that needs its own design

### The one thing nobody said but the architecture implies

The content-strategy and tech-architecture documents are detailed, thoughtful, and internally consistent. They're also both written *before a single learner has touched the product*. Every panellist, from different angles, arrived at the same meta-concern: **validate with real learners sooner, build less before you do.** The walking skeleton. The 12-hour MVP. The evaluator calibration corpus. These all point the same direction — close the feedback loop between the product and its users before investing in the full eleven-content-type, nine-module, container-orchestrated architecture.
