### Briefing: Andrej Karpathy’s View on Teaching for the Era of Software Pilotry

Andrej Karpathy describes a fundamental shift in the nature of software engineering and education, moving from manual labor to a state of **"AI psychosis"** where the human acts as a high-level orchestrator—a concept that aligns closely with the emerging discipline of **software pilotry**. In this new paradigm, "coding" is no longer the primary verb; instead, the practitioner **"manifests their will"** through a swarm of agents.

#### 1. From Instruction to "Skill Scripting"
Karpathy suggests that the traditional model of humans teaching other humans directly is being reshuffled. He argues that instead of creating tutorials or lectures for people, experts should focus on **explaining concepts to agents**. 
*   **The Teacher as Architect:** The educator's role shifts to "scripting the curriculum" by providing the specific "bits" of intuition or unique progression that an agent cannot spontaneously generate. 
*   **Agent as the Interface:** Once an agent "gets" a concept (like Karpathy’s 200-line "micro GPT"), it becomes the primary tutor, offering **infinite patience** and tailoring explanations to the learner's specific capability and language.

#### 2. Documentation for Agents, Not Humans
Relevant to the "pilot" who must oversee complex systems, Karpathy advocates for a change in how we document technology.
*   **Markdown over HTML:** He suggests moving away from HTML documentation intended for human eyes toward **Markdown documents designed for agents**. 
*   **Interpretable Intelligence:** If an agent can parse the documentation, it can act as the "router" that explains the system's different parts to the human pilot, facilitating more efficient oversight.

#### 3. Macro Actions and Verification
For a software pilot, the "skill issue" is no longer about syntax, but about **orchestration and verification**.
*   **Macro-Level Command:** Karpathy describes "mastery" as moving in **macro actions**—delegating entire functionalities to different agents (e.g., one for research, one for implementation, one for planning) rather than writing individual functions.
*   **The Verification Bottleneck:** As agents become the primary writers of code, the pilot’s value-add shifts to **objective evaluation**. Education must therefore focus on teaching how to define "objective criteria" and "metrics" so that autonomous loops (like "auto research") can function without the human being a bottleneck.

#### 4. Managing "Jagged Intelligence"
A critical component of teaching pilotry is acknowledging the **"jaggedness"** of current AI models. Karpathy observes that an agent can simultaneously behave like a brilliant PhD student and a 10-year-old, leading to nonsensical errors or "loops" that are totally wrong.
*   **Human Accountability:** Because the "probabilistic machine" can be confidently inconsistent, the pilot must be educated to **intervene and take responsibility** when the agent wastes compute or misinterprets nuance.
*   **Strategic Focus:** Karpathy suggests that learners should be "strategic about what they are actually spending time on," focusing on the **few bits of high-value judgment** that agents still struggle to master.

This perspective confirms the "Software Pilot Manifesto's" claim that education must evolve to teach the "machine beneath" (the core algorithms) and the "probabilistic machine above" (the LLM), with the human pilot serving as the **accountable overseer** of both.
